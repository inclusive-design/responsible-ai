title: Addressing shortage in human assistance
excerpt: AI can help extend access to support where human services are limited, providing on-demand assistance in areas such as communication, learning, and daily tasks.
opportunity: AI systems can help extend access to support when and  where trained professionals or human expertise are limited or unavailable. This includes interpretation, teaching, and guided interaction, where AI tools such as captioning systems, virtual tutors, and social robots can provide immediate, on-demand support. In low-resource settings, including low- and middle-income countries, these tools may help reduce service gaps where human assistance is scarce. These systems can also provide more consistent access outside of standard hours or geographic limits, which can create flexibility to support participation in education, services, and daily activities.
limitation: Quality and accuracy is uneven (and at times harmful due to bias and ableism); particularly for non-Western imagery, minority languages, disability representation, and culturally specific visuals.
examples:
  - title: Generative AI as a day-to-day support
    example: Many persons with disabilities utilize generative AI as a support or scaffold, for example, to summarize readings for comprehension, to assist with structuring thoughts from their own ideas, or to assist with reading or organizational barriers (associated with conditions such as dyslexia, ASD, and ADHD.).
    caution: There is a fine line between utilizing AI for support and scaffold purposes and having the AI complete the work on the user’s behalf. Policy should treat AI use as an access question first, as opposed to an integrity question. Using AI as accessibility support is not the same as appropriation, falsification, or plagiarism. In education and employment settings use of generative AI can be restricted or flagged, taking away supports that individuals may rely on for access.
  - title: Augmentative and alternative communication (AAC)
    example: Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) applications are important assistive technologies that support individuals with understanding and being understood in contexts where communication is vital to equitable participation and access. Contexts include, education, employment, banking, and social services such as healthcare.
    caution: Vocabulary and prediction features in Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) tools may shape or override how a person chooses to express themselves. This can result in responses that sound more typical or standardized, rather than reflecting the user’s own voice or intent. AAC tools should support, not replace, a person’s communication style and choices. In addition, support for less commonly used languages and dialects remains limited, which can reduce accessibility and make it harder for some users to communicate in ways that align with their identity and context.
  - title: Emotion-aware text-to-speech for richer audio access
    example: Text-to-speech is evolving to communicate in more natural sounding, expressive and less robotic language. Text-to-speech personalization allows for individual preferences in how content is communicated. Adjusting speed, expressiveness, gender or accent can support access and may increase engagement.
    caution: Voice options are dominated by English-language adult voices; minority languages and dialects are under-served. Emotional tone in synthesized speech can also be inaccurate, leading to a mismatch between how something sounds and what it is meant to communicate.
